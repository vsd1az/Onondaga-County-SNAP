SNAP provides New York residents with benefits as need expands

SNAP allows beneficiaries to purchase food for their households. (Getty Images)

New York's Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program has taken off since 2008. The need hasn't stopped growing since.

BY VALENTINA S. DIAZ


The Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program, otherwise known as SNAP, has been adopted in all 50 states across the United States. Among the states with the highest number of SNAP beneficiaries is New York, falling at number three on the list.

To begin to understand the need of the program, the program itself must be defined. SNAP is carried out by issuing “electronic benefits that can be used like cash to purchase food,” according to New York State. The program is on an eligibility basis requiring applicants to apply. Eligibility, based on income, is dependent on household size and (dis)ability.

Though a statewide issue, it is evident that the county of which Syracuse belongs to faces a food justice issue. Onondaga County has a food insecurity rate of 11.1 percent with 51,580 people falling under this percentage. In the county, there exists less than one third as many grocery stores as there does fast food restaurants. The result: a food desert.

Broadly, a food desert is constituted as an area with limited access to affordable and healthy food. Specifically, the United States Department of Agriculture defines a food desert as a community where the poverty rate is greater than or equal to 20 percent and where 33 percent or more of residents are more than one mile or ten miles—in urban and rural settings, respectively—from the nearest supermarket.

SNAP strives to alleviate this.
